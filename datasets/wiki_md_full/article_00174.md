Family of sea spiders

**Colossendeidae** is a [family](/wiki/Family_(biology) "Family (biology)") of [sea spiders](/wiki/Sea_spider "Sea spider") ([class](/wiki/Class_(biology) "Class (biology)") [Pycnogonida](/wiki/Pycnogonida "Pycnogonida")).[[1]](#cite_note-1) This family includes more than 100 [species](/wiki/Species "Species") distributed among six [genera](/wiki/Genus "Genus").[[2]](#cite_note-2) These sea spiders inhabit the deep sea mostly but are also found in shallower waters, especially in Antarctic and Arctic waters.[[3]](#cite_note-3)

## Description

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Colossendeidae&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Description")]

Most colossendeids can grow to giant sizes as adults, far larger than any other sea spiders. The largest sea spider, *[Colossendeis colossea](/wiki/Colossendeis_colossea "Colossendeis colossea")*, can reach a leg span of 70 cm,[[4]](#cite_note-4) whereas the subfamily Hedgpethiinae includes tiny species with leg span of less than 1 cm.[[5]](#cite_note-5) Like most sea spiders, colossendeids usually have four pairs of legs in adults, except for three species, *[Decolopoda australis](/wiki/Decolopoda "Decolopoda")*, *[D. qasimi](/wiki/Decolopoda "Decolopoda")*, and *[Pentacolossendeis reticulata](/wiki/Pentacolossendeis "Pentacolossendeis")*, with five leg pairs,[[6]](#cite_note-:2-6)[[7]](#cite_note-7) and one species, *[Dodecolopoda mawsoni](/wiki/Dodecolopoda "Dodecolopoda")*, with six leg pairs.[[8]](#cite_note-:3-8)[[9]](#cite_note-9)

Colossendeids typically have no [chelifores](/wiki/Chelicerae "Chelicerae"), except in the extra-legged species which have robust [pincers](/wiki/Chelae "Chelae") and 2-segmented scapes.[[8]](#cite_note-:3-8)[[6]](#cite_note-:2-6) The [palps](/wiki/Pedipalp "Pedipalp") and ovigers are present in both males and females, they are elongated with basal processes located very close to each other. The palps have 8 or 9 segments (historically 9 or 10 as the basal process was once thought to be a segment) while the ovigers have 10 segments.[[10]](#cite_note-10) Each oviger ends in a strong terminal claw and compound spines arranged in fields or rows on a well formed strigilis (hook-like distal segments).[[11]](#cite_note-:1-11)[[12]](#cite_note-:0-12) The proboscis is long, usually longer than the body. The body and legs are usually glabrous.[[12]](#cite_note-:0-12) The trunk segments are either segmented (Hedgpethiinae) or fused (Colossendeinae). The legs are very long, and the terminal main claws have no auxiliary claws. [gonopores](/wiki/Gonopore "Gonopore") represented on some or all legs.[[12]](#cite_note-:0-12)[[11]](#cite_note-:1-11)
