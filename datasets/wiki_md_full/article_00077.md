Species of flowering plant in the wood sorrel family

***Oxalis pes-caprae*** is a species of [tristylous](/wiki/Heterostyly "Heterostyly") yellow-flowering plant in the wood sorrel family [Oxalidaceae](/wiki/Oxalidaceae "Oxalidaceae"). ***Oxalis cernua*** is a less common synonym for this species.

It is commonly known as **African wood-sorrel**, **Bermuda buttercup,** **Bermuda sorrel**, **buttercup oxalis**, **Cape sorrel**, **English weed**, **goat's-foot**, **sourgrass**, **soursob** or **soursop**; **[Afrikaans](/wiki/Afrikaans_language "Afrikaans language"): *suring***; **[Arabic](/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"): *hommayda*** (حميضة).[[2]](#cite_note-isbn0-624-00533-X-2)

Some of the most common names for the plant reference its sour taste owing to [oxalic acid](/wiki/Oxalic_acid "Oxalic acid") present in its tissues. Indigenous to South Africa, the plant has become a [pest plant](/wiki/Pest_plant "Pest plant") in different parts of the world that is difficult to eradicate because of how it propagates through underground [bulbs](/wiki/Bulb "Bulb").

## Name

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Oxalis_pes-caprae&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Name")]

*Oxalis pes-caprae* is often called by the common name [*sour*grass](/wiki/Sourgrass "Sourgrass") due to its moderately [sour flavor](/wiki/Sour "Sour"). This sourness is caused by an exceptionally high content of [oxalic acid](/wiki/Oxalic_acid "Oxalic acid").

The specific epithet *pes-caprae* means 'goat's-foot', possibly in reference to the shape of the leaf.

## Description

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Oxalis_pes-caprae&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Description")]

The *Oxalis pes-caprae* flower is [actinomorphic](/wiki/Floral_symmetry "Floral symmetry"), with a [calyx](/wiki/Calyx_(botany) "Calyx (botany)") composed of five free or slightly fused [sepals](/wiki/Sepal "Sepal"), a [sympetalous](/wiki/Sympetaly "Sympetaly") [corolla](/wiki/Corolla_(flower) "Corolla (flower)") composed of five fused petals, an [androecium](/wiki/Androecium "Androecium") composed of ten free [stamens](/wiki/Stamen "Stamen") in two ranks, and a compound [pistil](/wiki/Gynoecium "Gynoecium"). Native populations in South Africa are [heterostylous](/wiki/Heterostyly "Heterostyly"), flowers of long-styled plants have a [stigma](/wiki/Stigma_(botany) "Stigma (botany)") held above the two ranks of stamens, mid-styled plants have the stigma in between the two ranks of stamens and short-styled plants have a stigma below both ranks of stamen. In the non-native range the plants largely reproduce vegetatively and many populations have only one style length and the plants never produce seed.[[3]](#cite_note-3)[[4]](#cite_note-4)

Like most African *Oxalis* species, it produces adventitious subterranean [propagules](/wiki/Propagule "Propagule"). These take the form of true [bulbs](/wiki/Bulb "Bulb") in botanical terms, which is unusual among [dicotyledons](/wiki/Dicotyledon "Dicotyledon"). *O. pes-caprae* produces small bulbs copiously, whereas most other African species produce fewer, larger bulbs.

## As an invasive species

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Oxalis_pes-caprae&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: As an invasive species")]

Indigenous to South Africa, *Oxalis pes-caprae* is an [invasive species](/wiki/Invasive_species "Invasive species") and [noxious weed](/wiki/Noxious_weed "Noxious weed") in many other parts of the world, including the United States (particularly coastal California),[[5]](#cite_note-5) Europe,[[6]](#cite_note-6) [The Middle East](/wiki/The_Middle_East "The Middle East") and Australia.[[7]](#cite_note-7)

### Control

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Oxalis_pes-caprae&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Control")]

The plant has a reputation for being very difficult to eliminate by mechanical methods once it has spread over an area of land.[[8]](#cite_note-8) The weed propagates largely through its underground bulbs and this is one reason why it is so difficult to eradicate, as pulling up the plant, even with the roots, can leave some of the bulbs behind. Soil in which the plant has grown is generally contaminated with many small bulbs.

Kluge & Claassens (1990) reported a potential biocontrol agent using *[Klugeana](/wiki/Klugeana "Klugeana") philoxalis*, a larval feeder on shoots of *O. pes-caprae*.[[9]](#cite_note-9)[[10]](#cite_note-10)

*O. pes-caprae* is also a host to [broomrape](/wiki/Broomrape "Broomrape"), though it is not clear that that is of significance as a control agent.[[11]](#cite_note-11)

Two studies have reported successful control using two different non-selective, consumer-grade herbicides. Imazapyr[[12]](#cite_note-12) applied in late March 2016 at low concentration appeared to completely prevent re-sprouting of *O. pes-caprae* bulbs in the San Francisco area.[[13]](#cite_note-13) He reports "The difficulty of control of Bermuda buttercup has led some to consider control impractical... The use of herbicides may also have high efficacy for habitat restoration of infested fields and woodlands." Glyphosate[[14]](#cite_note-14) was used successfully on a Mediterranean island.[[15]](#cite_note-15) They report, "The use of glyphosate was found to be the only efficient control method, leading to a significant decrease in *O. pes-caprae* cover as well as to a recovery in native species cover and richness. The effect of the other control methods was negligible."

## Hazards

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Oxalis_pes-caprae&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Hazards")]

The leaves or roots are used in some parts of the world as a food,[[16]](#cite_note-16)[[17]](#cite_note-17) although the high levels of calcium oxalate in the plant may bear some risks. Such risks can be reduced if the plant parts are boiled first.[[18]](#cite_note-18) [Oxalic acid](/wiki/Oxalic_acid "Oxalic acid") is toxic in large quantities, a concern in regions such as southern Australia where *Oxalis pes-caprae* grows invasively in enormous quantities and in high densities.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*] Various sources suggest that *oxalis* ingestion causes calcium oxalate kidney stones, but clinical experience and physiological considerations make it unlikely that any realistic intake of *Oxalis* would affect human liability to [kidney stones](/wiki/Kidney_stone "Kidney stone").[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*] Accordingly, some Australian references to the hazards of *oxalis* to livestock tend to be dismissive of this risk.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*]

However, in spite of its comparatively benign nature, where it has become dominant in pastures, as sometimes happens outside South Africa, *Oxalis pes-caprae* certainly can cause dramatic stock losses. For example, when hungry stock, such as sheep released just after being shorn, are let out to graze in a lush growth of *Oxalis pes-caprae*, they may gorge on the plant, with fatal results, as has been found in South Australia at least.[[19]](#cite_note-19)

Such stock fatalities patently have little logical connection with the presence or absence of oxalate kidney stones. For one thing, the fatal effects on sheep are far too rapid to result from the growth of bulk kidney stones. The plant has been found to be nutritious, but too acidic to be good fodder, largely being left untouched by grazing stock. When stock do consume large quantities, the effects typically involve death in several weeks with symptoms suggesting chronic oxalate poisoning, including [tetany](/wiki/Tetany "Tetany"), or sudden death with extensive renal damage.[[20]](#cite_note-watt-20) Such damage suggests the twofold effect of calcium immobilisation (the tetany) and the formation of Calcium Oxalate Monohydrate [raphides](/wiki/Raphide "Raphide") in the kidney tissue.[[21]](#cite_note-pmid19852621-21) The histotoxic effects of the raphides in kidney have by now been investigated.[[22]](#cite_note-22)

Oxalis poisoning of stock is not a serious [forage](/wiki/Forage "Forage") concern in South African pastures, unless exceptionally favoured by [overgrazing](/wiki/Overgrazing "Overgrazing").

## Uses

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Oxalis_pes-caprae&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Uses")]

The plant is palatable and in modest quantities is reasonably harmless to humans and livestock. In South Africa it is a traditional ingredient in dishes such as [waterblommetjiebredie](/wiki/Waterblommetjiebredie "Waterblommetjiebredie") ('water flower stew').[[23]](#cite_note-isbn0-7981-4919-1-23)

The plant has been used in various ways as a source of oxalic acid, as food, and in folk medicine. The raw bulbs have been used to deal with [tapeworm](/wiki/Tapeworm "Tapeworm") and possibly other worms. The plant has been used as a diuretic, possibly hazardously. The lateral underground runners, which tend to be fleshy, have been eaten raw or boiled and served with milk.[[20]](#cite_note-watt-20) The golden petals can be used to produce a yellow [dye](/wiki/Dye "Dye").[[24]](#cite_note-24)
