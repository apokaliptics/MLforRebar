American documentary filmmaker and civil rights activist

**Judy Richardson** is an American [documentary filmmaker](/wiki/Documentary_film "Documentary film") and [civil rights activist](/wiki/Civil_rights_movement "Civil rights movement"). She was Distinguished Visiting Lecturer of [Africana Studies](/wiki/Africana_studies "Africana studies") at [Brown University](/wiki/Brown_University "Brown University").[[1]](#cite_note-1)

## Early life and education

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Judy_Richardson&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early life and education")]

Richardson was born in [Tarrytown, New York](/wiki/Tarrytown,_New_York "Tarrytown, New York").[[2]](#cite_note-2) She attended Washington Irving Jr. High.[[3]](#cite_note-3) She then enrolled at [Swarthmore College](/wiki/Swarthmore_College "Swarthmore College") in [Pennsylvania](/wiki/Pennsylvania "Pennsylvania") in 1962 on a full [scholarship](/wiki/Scholarship "Scholarship").[[4]](#cite_note-Swarthmore-2014-4)[[5]](#cite_note-Columbus-5)

## Activism

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Judy_Richardson&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Activism")]

During Richardson's freshman year at Swarthmore in 1962–1963, she joined the Swarthmore Political Action Committee (SPAC), a [Students for a Democratic Society](/wiki/Students_for_a_Democratic_Society "Students for a Democratic Society") (SDS) affiliate.

Richardson was an early participant with the [Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee](/wiki/Student_Nonviolent_Coordinating_Committee "Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee") (SNCC) from 1963 to 1966. During her time with SNCC, [Ella Baker](/wiki/Ella_Baker "Ella Baker") was her mentor.[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6)[[7]](#cite_note-NHD-7) In 1963, Richardson traveled on weekends, with other Swarthmore SPAC volunteers, to assist the [Cambridge, Maryland](/wiki/Cambridge,_Maryland "Cambridge, Maryland") community in [desegregating](/wiki/Desegregation_in_the_United_States "Desegregation in the United States") public accommodations.[[5]](#cite_note-Columbus-5)[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8)[[9]](#cite_note-9) The [Cambridge Movement](/wiki/Cambridge_movement_(civil_rights) "Cambridge movement (civil rights)") was led by [Gloria Richardson](/wiki/Gloria_Richardson "Gloria Richardson") with assistance from SNCC field secretaries, including [Baltimore](/wiki/Baltimore "Baltimore") native Reggie Robinson.[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8) In 1963, Richardson joined a SNCC organized [sit-in](/wiki/Sit-in "Sit-in") at a [Toddle House](/wiki/Toddle_House "Toddle House") in [Atlanta](/wiki/Atlanta "Atlanta").[[7]](#cite_note-NHD-7) Richardson eventually joined the SNCC staff at the national office in Atlanta, where she worked closely with [James Forman](/wiki/James_Forman "James Forman"), [Ruby Doris Smith-Robinson](/wiki/Ruby_Doris_Smith-Robinson "Ruby Doris Smith-Robinson"), and [Julian Bond](/wiki/Julian_Bond "Julian Bond").[[5]](#cite_note-Columbus-5)[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8)[[10]](#cite_note-Judy_Richardson_Personal_Papers-10)

Richardson relocated to [Mississippi](/wiki/Mississippi "Mississippi") during 1964 [Freedom Summer](/wiki/Freedom_Summer "Freedom Summer") after the SNCC national office moved there.[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8) She worked with SNCC during their effort that summer to register African American voters in Mississippi, joining [Amzie Moore](/wiki/Amzie_Moore "Amzie Moore"), [Bob Moses](/wiki/Bob_Moses_(activist) "Bob Moses (activist)"), [Curtis Hayes](/wiki/Curtis_Muhammad "Curtis Muhammad"), and [Hollis Watkins](/wiki/Hollis_Watkins "Hollis Watkins").[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6)[[7]](#cite_note-NHD-7) Richardson also worked in SNCC's projects in [Lowndes County, Alabama](/wiki/Lowndes_County,_Alabama "Lowndes County, Alabama") (with [Stokely Carmichael](/wiki/Stokely_Carmichael "Stokely Carmichael")/Kwame Ture and others) and in Southwest Georgia.[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8) Richardson became Julian Bond's office manager in 1965, during his successful first campaign for the [Georgia House of Representatives](/wiki/Georgia_House_of_Representatives "Georgia House of Representatives").[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8)[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6) She also organized a northern [Freedom School](/wiki/Freedom_Schools "Freedom Schools"), bringing together young activists from SNCC's northern and southern projects.[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8)

## Drum and Spear Bookstore

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Judy_Richardson&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Drum and Spear Bookstore")]

In 1968, shortly after the assassination of [Martin Luther King Jr.](/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr. "Martin Luther King Jr."), Richardson and other former SNCC staffers founded Drum and Spear Bookstore in [Washington, D.C.](/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.") It became the largest Black bookstore in the country, with Richardson as the children's editor of Drum and Spear Press.[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8) Richardson said about the bookstore's name that the drum symbolized "communications within the diaspora" while the spear suggested "whatever else might be necessary for the liberation of the people."[[11]](#cite_note-11)

In 1970, [Howard University](/wiki/Howard_University "Howard University")'s *[Journal of Negro Education](/wiki/Journal_of_Negro_Education "Journal of Negro Education")* published Richardson's essay on [racism](/wiki/Racism "Racism") in Black children's books.[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8)

## Later years

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Judy_Richardson&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Later years")]

Richardson attended [Columbia University](/wiki/Columbia_University "Columbia University") and received her degree from [Antioch College](/wiki/Antioch_College "Antioch College") in General Studies.[[12]](#cite_note-12) In 2012, Richardson received an honorary degree from Swarthmore and spoke at the 2012 commencement ceremony.[[4]](#cite_note-Swarthmore-2014-4)

In 2019, Richardson was the keynote speaker for [National History Day](/wiki/National_History_Day "National History Day").[[7]](#cite_note-NHD-7) In September 2020 she was featured on the [USA Today](/wiki/USA_Today "USA Today") Storytellers Project Live.[[13]](#cite_note-13)

Richardson serves on the board of directors of the SNCC Legacy Project, which preserves records of Black activism past and present.[[5]](#cite_note-Columbus-5)[[14]](#cite_note-14)

Richardson was recognized as a Local Hero and interviewed by Congressman [Jamie Raskin](/wiki/Jamie_Raskin "Jamie Raskin") on January 12, 2024. The interview was posted on YouTube, episode 192 of the Local Hero Video Series.[[15]](#cite_note-15)

## Films and publications

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Judy_Richardson&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Films and publications")]

Starting in the late 1970s, Richardson became an early researcher, series associate producer, and content advisor for the series *[Eyes on the Prize](/wiki/Eyes_on_the_Prize "Eyes on the Prize"),* which [Henry Hampton](/wiki/Henry_Hampton "Henry Hampton") executive produced through his company Blackside.[[5]](#cite_note-Columbus-5)[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6)[[16]](#cite_note-Richardson-Blackside-16)[[17]](#cite_note-17) *Eyes on the Prize* was a 14-hour documentary series on the history of the [American civil rights movement](/wiki/Civil_rights_movement "Civil rights movement"), broadcast on [PBS](/wiki/PBS "PBS") in 1987 and 1990. The series was nominated for an [Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature](/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Documentary_Feature "Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature") in 1988.[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6) Richardson later co-produced Blackside's 1994 [Emmy](/wiki/Emmy_Awards "Emmy Awards") and [Peabody Award](/wiki/Peabody_Awards "Peabody Awards")-winning documentary, *Malcolm X: Make It Plain* (for PBS's The American Experience).[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6)[[16]](#cite_note-Richardson-Blackside-16)

Serving as a senior producer for [Northern Light Productions](/wiki/Northern_Light_Productions "Northern Light Productions") in Boston, Richardson produced historical documentaries for broadcast and museums, with a focus on African American historical events, including: a one-hour documentary called *Scarred Justice: Orangeburg Massacre 1968* (South Carolina) for PBS;[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6) two History Channel documentaries on slavery and slave resistance; and installations for, among others, the [National Park Service](/wiki/National_Park_Service "National Park Service")'s Little Rock Nine Visitor's Center, the [National Underground Railroad Freedom Center](/wiki/National_Underground_Railroad_Freedom_Center "National Underground Railroad Freedom Center"), the [New York State Historical Society's](/wiki/New-York_Historical_Society "New-York Historical Society") "Slavery in New York" exhibit, and the [Paul Laurence Dunbar House](/wiki/Paul_Laurence_Dunbar_House "Paul Laurence Dunbar House").[[16]](#cite_note-Richardson-Blackside-16)

Richardson co-edited *Hands on the Freedom Plow: Personal Accounts By Women in SNCC* published by University of Illinois Press.[[8]](#cite_note-Richardson-WashUniv-8) The book won the [NAACP Image Award for Outstanding Literary Work, Nonfiction](/wiki/NAACP_Image_Award_for_Outstanding_Literary_Work,_Nonfiction "NAACP Image Award for Outstanding Literary Work, Nonfiction") in 2011.[[5]](#cite_note-Columbus-5)[[6]](#cite_note-UChicago-6)
