Former Egyptian Minister of Justice

**Ahmed Mekki** (born 1941) was the [Minister of Justice](/wiki/Minister_of_Justice "Minister of Justice") of [Egypt](/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") from 2 August 2012 until he submitted his resignation to President Morsi on 20 April 2013.[[1]](#cite_note-1) He was a member of the [Qandil Cabinet](/wiki/Qandil_Cabinet "Qandil Cabinet").[[2]](#cite_note-2) Mekki was one of the independent ministers in the cabinet.[[3]](#cite_note-acc-3) He is the brother of the former vice president [Mahmoud Mekki](/wiki/Mahmoud_Mekki "Mahmoud Mekki"), who resigned from office on 22 December 2012.[[4]](#cite_note-voa-4)[[5]](#cite_note-5)

## Early life and education

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Ahmed_Mekki_(politician)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early life and education")]

Mekki was born in 1941.[[6]](#cite_note-ce-6) He studied law at [Alexandria University](/wiki/Alexandria_University "Alexandria University") and graduated in 1961.[[6]](#cite_note-ce-6)

## Career

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Ahmed_Mekki_(politician)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Career")]

Mekki is the former deputy head of the Court of Cassation, Egypt’s highest appeals court.[[7]](#cite_note-afp-7)[[8]](#cite_note-psyn-8) He was also the chairman of the fact-finding Committee in the [Egyptian Judges Club](/wiki/Egyptian_Judges_Club "Egyptian Judges Club").[[3]](#cite_note-acc-3)

On 2 August 2012, he began to serve as minister of justice in the cabinet led by prime minister [Hesham Qandil](/wiki/Hesham_Qandil "Hesham Qandil"), replacing Adel Abdel Hamid.[[9]](#cite_note-alahram711-9) Although Mekki was an independent member of the cabinet, he is close to [the Muslim Brotherhood](/wiki/The_Muslim_Brotherhood "The Muslim Brotherhood").[[10]](#cite_note-eldin3aug-10) Mekki resigned from office on 20 April 2013.[[11]](#cite_note-say21apr-11) His resignation was due to pressure from both the opposition and Brotherhood supporters.[[12]](#cite_note-aze21apr-12) In a reshuffle of May 2013, Ahmed Sulaiman was appointed minister of justice, succeeding Mekki in the post.[[13]](#cite_note-voa7may-13)[[14]](#cite_note-aahram-14)

### Views

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Ahmed_Mekki_(politician)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Views")]

Mekki is a strong supporter of judicial independence.[[8]](#cite_note-psyn-8) He was known as "a reformist judge", and "the revolution’s representative" in Qandil’s government.[[8]](#cite_note-psyn-8)[[15]](#cite_note-ebus5mar-15) After his appointment, Mekki argued that Egypt is an Islamic state governed by Islamic traditions.[[16]](#cite_note-mcn-16)
