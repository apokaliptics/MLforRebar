Cantata by J.S. Bach

***Tönet, ihr Pauken! Erschallet, Trompeten!*** (Resound, ye drums![[a]](#cite_note-1) Ring out, ye trumpets!),[[1]](#cite_note-bc-2) **BWV 214**, is a [secular cantata](/wiki/List_of_secular_cantatas_by_Johann_Sebastian_Bach "List of secular cantatas by Johann Sebastian Bach") by [Johann Sebastian Bach](/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach "Johann Sebastian Bach"), composed in 1733 for the birthday of [Maria Josepha](/wiki/Maria_Josepha_of_Austria "Maria Josepha of Austria"), Queen of Poland and Electress of [Saxony](/wiki/Saxony "Saxony"). Classified in published editions as a [dramma per musica](/wiki/Dramma_per_musica "Dramma per musica"),[[2]](#cite_note-Breitkopf-3) it is based on a [libretto](/wiki/Libretto "Libretto") by an unknown author. The piece has the dedicatee addressed by allegorical figures representing Roman and Greek goddesses of war and peace. It is structured as nine [movements](/wiki/Movement_(music) "Movement (music)"), and scored for four vocal parts and a festive [Baroque orchestra](/wiki/Baroque_instruments "Baroque instruments") with trumpets, timpani, flutes,[[b]](#cite_note-4) oboes and strings. Choral movements frame a series of alternating [recitatives](/wiki/Recitative "Recitative") and [arias](/wiki/Aria "Aria"). Bach led the first performance with the [Collegium Musicum](/wiki/Collegium_Musicum#Leipzig "Collegium Musicum") at the [Zimmermannsches Caffeehaus](/wiki/Zimmermannsches_Caffeehaus "Zimmermannsches Caffeehaus") on 8 December 1733.

*Tönet, ihr Pauken!* was first published by the [Bach Gesellschaft](/wiki/Bach_Gesellschaft "Bach Gesellschaft") in 1887, as part of the first complete edition of Bach's works. It appeared in the *[Neue Bach-Ausgabe](/wiki/Neue_Bach-Ausgabe "Neue Bach-Ausgabe")* in 1962. It was first recorded by the [Kantorei Barmen-Gemarke](/wiki/Kantorei_Barmen-Gemarke "Kantorei Barmen-Gemarke") in 1961, and subsequently, as part of their complete sets of the secular cantatas, by the [Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra & Choir](/wiki/Amsterdam_Baroque_Orchestra_%26_Choir "Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra & Choir") and the [Bach Collegium Japan](/wiki/Bach_Collegium_Japan "Bach Collegium Japan").

Bach reused the music of the two choral movements and two arias a year later in his *[Christmas Oratorio](/wiki/Christmas_Oratorio "Christmas Oratorio")*, notably for the movements opening Part I, *[Jauchzet, frohlocket!](/wiki/Jauchzet,_frohlocket!_BWV_248_I "Jauchzet, frohlocket! BWV 248 I")*, and Part III, *Herrscher des Himmels*.

## Background and text

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background and text")]

Bach composed *Tönet, ihr Pauken!* in 1733, ten years after he became *[Thomaskantor](/wiki/Thomaskantor "Thomaskantor")* in Leipzig, director of music in major churches in the town in the [Electorate of Saxony](/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony "Electorate of Saxony"). That year, [Augustus](/wiki/Augustus_III_of_Poland "Augustus III of Poland") succeeded his father as Elector of Saxony. Bach had hopes of being appointed composer to his court which was based in [Dresden](/wiki/Dresden "Dresden"). He dedicated his [Kyrie–Gloria Mass in B minor](/wiki/Mass_for_the_Dresden_court_(Bach) "Mass for the Dresden court (Bach)") to the elector, a work which was to become the [Kyrie and Gloria](/wiki/Mass_in_B_minor_structure#No._1_Missa "Mass in B minor structure") of his [Mass in B minor](/wiki/Mass_in_B_minor "Mass in B minor").[[3]](#cite_note-5) Bach also composed cantatas in honour of the elector's family, [*Laßt uns sorgen, laßt uns wachen*, BWV 213](/wiki/La%C3%9Ft_uns_sorgen,_la%C3%9Ft_uns_wachen,_BWV_213 "Laßt uns sorgen, laßt uns wachen, BWV 213"), performed on 5 September 1733, the eleventh birthday of the son of the elector, and another for his wife, [Maria Josepha](/wiki/Maria_Josepha_of_Austria "Maria Josepha of Austria").[[4]](#cite_note-6) Bach composed *Tönet, ihr Pauken!* to honour the 34th birthday of Maria Josepha on 8 December.[[5]](#cite_note-Dürr-7) It is also known by the description **Glückwunschkantate zum Geburtstage der Königin** (Congratulation cantata for the Queen's birthday), although Maria Josepha was not crowned Queen of Poland until January 1734.[[6]](#cite_note-Wessel-8)

The [librettist](/wiki/Libretto "Libretto") of the text is unknown.[[7]](#cite_note-bd-9) The action in the cantata is between four allegorical figures for goddesses from [Roman](/wiki/Roman_mythology "Roman mythology") and [Greek mythology](/wiki/Greek_mythology "Greek mythology"): [Bellona](/wiki/Bellona_(goddess) "Bellona (goddess)"), a Roman goddess of war, [Pallas](/wiki/Athena "Athena"), a Greek goddess of wisdom and warfare, [Irene](/wiki/Eirene_(goddess) "Eirene (goddess)"), a Greek goddess of peace, and [Fama](/wiki/Fama_(goddess) "Fama (goddess)"), a Roman goddess of fame and rumor.[[7]](#cite_note-bd-9) Bach led the [Collegium Musicum](/wiki/Collegium_Musicum#Leipzig "Collegium Musicum") at the first performance at the [Zimmermannsches Caffeehaus](/wiki/Zimmermannsches_Caffeehaus "Zimmermannsches Caffeehaus") in [Leipzig](/wiki/Leipzig "Leipzig") on 8 December 1733.[[7]](#cite_note-bd-9)

Bach used some of the music a year later for his *[Christmas Oratorio](/wiki/Christmas_Oratorio "Christmas Oratorio")*:[[7]](#cite_note-bd-9) including the opening movement with a timpani solo which is used to begin [Part I](/wiki/Jauchzet,_frohlocket!_BWV_248_I "Jauchzet, frohlocket! BWV 248 I"), its closing movement which became the opening of [Part III](/wiki/Christmas_Oratorio#Part_III "Christmas Oratorio"), and two arias.[[5]](#cite_note-Dürr-7)

*Tönet, ihr Pauken* is counted among Bach's works for celebrations of the [Leipzig University](/wiki/Leipzig_University "Leipzig University"), *Festmusiken zu Leipziger Universitätsfeiern*.[[8]](#cite_note-Timm-10)

### Publication

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Publication")]

*Tönet, ihr Pauken!* was published in 1887 as part of the first complete edition of the composer's works, by the [Bach Gesellschaft](/wiki/Bach_Gesellschaft "Bach Gesellschaft").[[7]](#cite_note-bd-9) The *Christmas Oratorio* had already appeared in the same series in 1856.[[9]](#cite_note-BG-11) The cantata was published as part of the *[Neue Bach-Ausgabe](/wiki/Neue_Bach-Ausgabe "Neue Bach-Ausgabe")* in 1962, edited by [Werner Neumann](/wiki/Werner_Neumann "Werner Neumann").[[7]](#cite_note-bd-9)

## Music

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Music")]

### Scoring and structure

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Scoring and structure")]

The cantata is structured in nine movements, with choral outer movements and alternating [recitatives](/wiki/Recitative "Recitative") and [arias](/wiki/Aria "Aria") as inner movements. The work features four vocal soloists who represent allegorical figures: Bellona ([soprano](/wiki/Soprano "Soprano")), Pallas ([alto](/wiki/Alto "Alto")), Irene ([tenor](/wiki/Tenor "Tenor")), and Fama ([bass](/wiki/Bass_(voice_type) "Bass (voice type)")). It is further scored for [four-part choir](/wiki/SATB "SATB") ([SATB](/wiki/SATB "SATB")) and a [Baroque instrumental](/wiki/Baroque_instruments "Baroque instruments") ensemble of three [trumpets](/wiki/Trumpet "Trumpet") (Tr), [timpani](/wiki/Timpani "Timpani"), two [flutes](/wiki/Flauto_traverso "Flauto traverso") (Ft), two [oboes](/wiki/Oboe "Oboe") (Ob), [oboe d'amore](/wiki/Oboe_d%27amore "Oboe d'amore") (Oa), two [violins](/wiki/Violin "Violin") (Vl), [viola](/wiki/Viola "Viola") (Va) and [basso continuo](/wiki/Basso_continuo "Basso continuo").[[7]](#cite_note-bd-9)[[10]](#cite_note-12)

In the following table, the scoring follows the *[Neue Bach-Ausgabe](/wiki/Neue_Bach-Ausgabe "Neue Bach-Ausgabe")* (*New Bach Edition*). The [keys](/wiki/Key_(music) "Key (music)") and [time signatures](/wiki/Time_signature "Time signature") are from [Alfred Dürr](/wiki/Alfred_D%C3%BCrr "Alfred Dürr"), and use the symbol for common time.[[5]](#cite_note-Dürr-7) The continuo, played throughout, is not shown. The timpani always play with the trumpets.

### Movements

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Movements")]

#### 1

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: 1")]

The opening chorus, "Tönet, ihr Pauken! Erschallet, Trompeten!" (Sound, you drums! Ring forth, trumpets!),[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) is a complex long [da capo](/wiki/Da_capo "Da capo") form (ABA). Unusually for Bach, it opens with a timpani solo.[[12]](#cite_note-Rathey-14) It is followed by the trumpets, and then by the strings and woodwinds.[[12]](#cite_note-Rathey-14) The extended instrumental [ritornello](/wiki/Ritornello "Ritornello") presents the musical material of the whole movement.[[12]](#cite_note-Rathey-14) The voices enter in [unison](/wiki/Unison "Unison"), first imitating timpani, then trumpets.[[12]](#cite_note-Rathey-14) In the following section, the vocal lines are mostly [homophonic](/wiki/Homophony "Homophony") and sometimes imitative, while the instrumental forces drive the movement.[[12]](#cite_note-Rathey-14) The second section is a modified repeat of the first. The middle section (B) of the movement also has two elements, an imitative passage in [B minor](/wiki/B_minor "B minor") accompanied only by the strings, and a mostly homophonic part with strings and woodwinds, after which A is repeated completely.[[12]](#cite_note-Rathey-14)

Musicologist Julian Mincham notes that "the sweeping exhilaration of this movement is impossible to describe in words".[[13]](#cite_note-min-15) Bach reused the movement as the opening movement of his *Christmas Oratorio*, "Jauchzet, frohlocket" (Shout for joy, exult).[[12]](#cite_note-Rathey-14) The voices imitate the sound of timpani and trumpets even with the new text.[[5]](#cite_note-Dürr-7)

#### 2

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: 2")]

Irene is the first to address the dedicatee. The tenor recitative, "Heut ist der Tag, wo jeder sich erfreuen mag" (This is the day, when everyone can be happy),[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) conveys imagery.[[13]](#cite_note-min-15)

#### 3

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: 3")]

Bellona sings an aria, "Blast die wohlgegriffnen Flöten" (Blow the well-handled flutes).[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) Matching the text, the soprano is accompanied by two [obbligato](/wiki/Obbligato "Obbligato") flutes. The aria is in three parts, with the third part similar to the first but on different text.[[6]](#cite_note-Wessel-8)

#### 4

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: 4")]

The soprano recitative, "Mein knallendes Metall" (My clashing metal),[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) represents the "clashing of arms" and the battlefield, related to the goddess of war.[[13]](#cite_note-min-15)

#### 5

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: 5")]

Pallas refers to the [muses](/wiki/Muses "Muses") in an alto aria, Fromme Musen! meine Glieder! (Virtuous Muses! My followers!).[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) It is the only movement in a [minor key](/wiki/Minor_scale "Minor scale"), accompanied by an obbligato oboe d'amore.[[13]](#cite_note-min-15)

Bach reused the music, [transposed](/wiki/Transposition_(music) "Transposition (music)") for a tenor, in *[Und es waren Hirten in derselben Gegend](/wiki/Und_es_waren_Hirten_in_derselben_Gegend,_BWV_248_II "Und es waren Hirten in derselben Gegend, BWV 248 II")*, Part II of his *Christmas Oratorio*, as the aria "Frohe Hirten, eilt, ach eilet", with an obbligato flute, No. 15, in which the shepherds are called to rush to the manger.[[13]](#cite_note-min-15)

#### 6

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: 6")]

The alto recitative, "Unsre Königin im Lande" (Our Queen of the land!),[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) is accompanied by strings playing [chords](/wiki/Chord_(music) "Chord (music)").[[13]](#cite_note-min-15)

#### 7

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: 7")]

Fama is the last character to appear, singing in a bass [da capo aria](/wiki/Da_capo_aria "Da capo aria"), "Kron und Preis gekrönter Damen" (Crown and trophy of royal ladies).[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) The voice is complemented by a majestic obbligato trumpet line that underlines the "triumph, dignity and splendor" of the queen. The text focuses on the dual themes of fame and virtue.[[13]](#cite_note-min-15) In this aria, the queen is addressed directly for the first time, "Königin! Mit deinem Namen füll ich diesen Kreis der Welt" (Queen! With your name I flood the entire world).[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) The [fanfares](/wiki/Fanfare "Fanfare") of the trumpet are used to illustrate her royal appearance.[[6]](#cite_note-Wessel-8)

In his *Christmas Oratorio*, Bach used the music in the aria "Großer Herr und starker König" (No. 8).[[6]](#cite_note-Wessel-8)

#### 8

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: 8")]

The bass recitative, "So dringe in das weite Erdenrund" (Thus throughout the whole round earth),[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) is accompanied by woodwinds.[[13]](#cite_note-min-15)

#### 9

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: 9")]

The cantata is closed with the chorus "Blühet, ihr Linden in Sachsen, wie Zedern" (Bloom, you Saxon lindens, like cedars),[[11]](#cite_note-Dellal-13) a dance-like chorus.[[13]](#cite_note-min-15) Three voices enter successively with parts of the text, first the tenor, the only voice without an aria, then the soprano followed by the alto. All voices repeat the text in homophony, with the first theme in the alto. After an interlude, the process is repeated for the other half of the text.[[6]](#cite_note-Wessel-8)

Bach reused the music of this movement in his *Christmas Oratorio* to frame Part III, "Herrscher des Himmels" (No. 36).[[6]](#cite_note-Wessel-8)

## Performances and recordings

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=T%C3%B6net,_ihr_Pauken!_Erschallet,_Trompeten!_BWV_214&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Performances and recordings")]

The cantata was performed at the [BBC Proms](/wiki/BBC_Proms "BBC Proms") in 1997 by the [Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra & Choir](/wiki/Amsterdam_Baroque_Orchestra_%26_Choir "Amsterdam Baroque Orchestra & Choir") conducted by [Ton Koopman](/wiki/Ton_Koopman "Ton Koopman").[[14]](#cite_note-BBC-16) In contrast, the complete *Christmas Oratorio* was given in the same concert series in 1972 conducted by [Paul Steinitz](/wiki/Paul_Steinitz "Paul Steinitz"), and one aria had been performed as early as 1908.[[15]](#cite_note-BBC2-17)

Recordings have been made by several ensembles, including:[[1]](#cite_note-bc-2)

* [Kantorei Barmen-Gemarke](/wiki/Kantorei_Barmen-Gemarke "Kantorei Barmen-Gemarke"), [Helmut Kahlhöfer](/wiki/Helmut_Kahlh%C3%B6fer "Helmut Kahlhöfer"), *J. S. Bach: Cantatas BWV 207a & BWV 214*, Cantate 1961

* [Bach Collegium Japan](/wiki/Bach_Collegium_Japan "Bach Collegium Japan"), [Masaaki Suzuki](/wiki/Masaaki_Suzuki "Masaaki Suzuki"). *J. S Bach: Secular Cantatas Vol. 5*, [BIS](/wiki/BIS_Records "BIS Records") 2014
